Abstract

Cross-embodiment navigation is a key challenge in embodied intelligence. Due to differences in size and traversability, the same visual observation may imply different actions for different agents, making prediction ambiguous when relying solely on vision. Existing studies mainly rely on reinforcement learning, while imitation-learning-based approaches remain limited, restricting the pretraining and real-world fine-tuning of navigation models. To address this issue, we propose an imitation-learning-based multimodal embodiment-aware navigation framework with a multi-stage modular design. In pretraining, we construct a cross-embodiment navigation dataset from Internet videos and introduce embodiment geometry as conditional tokens to reduce action ambiguity under the same observation. In fine-tuning, we design a multimodal information injection mechanism based on a decoupled architecture. Specifically, we use a trajectory augmentation module to generate high-risk samples, which are then used to separately train a distance perception module and a trajectory correction module, thereby explicitly incorporating embodiment geometry for safe navigation. Experimental results show that the proposed method effectively improves navigation performance across different embodiment geometries, demonstrating the effectiveness of incorporating embodiment geometry into embodied navigation.

Method

Figure 1. The system extracts transferable features from egocentric image sequences and proprioceptive signals, then predicts continuous actions directly for efficient navigation control.

Unified Cross-Embodiment Data

We build a scalable data pipeline that aligns egocentric navigation trajectories collected from robots with very different dynamics and morphology.

Robust Spatiotemporal Modeling

Visual history and proprioceptive priors are fused to produce stable action predictions under perturbations, motion variation, and scene changes.

Single-Step Low-Latency Inference

The policy avoids long iterative generation and is therefore suitable for high-frequency control loops on real robotic hardware.
